Integrating benchmarking into the development process

We're working on integrating benchmarking into our development process to ensure that our models are performing optimally and to track improvements over time.

After each iteration, we run our pipeline on a set of benchmark datasets and compare the results to a reference dataset to evaluate the performance of the model. We also compare the performance of different models against each other to identify whether a new model is an improvement over the previous one. This is done entirely in Python, using a single script, i.e. without deploying the model to the iPhone. This means that, although the benchmarking is very useful, it is not a perfect representation of the performance of the model on the iPhone, and user testing is still required.

Benchmark Datasets

We've been working on our benchmarking datasets of recorded speech.

We've created audio/transcript datasets for the following use cases:

  • Bar/Crowd noise.

    This is the main use case, a conversation in a public place with other people speaking nearby.

  • TV as unwanted background.

    A conversation indoors with a TV/radio on in the background.

  • TV speech.

    A TV, which you're trying to listen to, with home noises.

  • Indoors home

    A conversation, with home noises (transients and continuous).

  • Indoors transport

    A conversation in a car/train/plane (continuous).

  • Indoors quiet

    A conversation in a quiet room.

  • Unwanted music (without vocals)

    A conversation indoors, with unwanted background music.

For each of the datasets, we've created a reference dataset, representing the ideal output. Running the benchmark on each of these datasets without any noise reduction (i.e. not using the app) gives us a baseline.

Benchmark Metrics

The metrics we're using initially are:

  • PESQ (Perceptual Evaluation of Speech Quality)

    • PESQ is a widely used metric for assessing the quality of speech as perceived by human listeners. It compares the original and degraded speech signals to provide a score between -0.5 and 4.5, with higher scores indicating better quality

  • STOI (Short-Time Objective Intelligibility)

    • STOI measures the intelligibility of speech, particularly in noisy environments. It compares the clean and degraded speech signals to provide a score between 0 and 1, with higher scores indicating better intelligibility

  • WER (Word Error Rate)

    WER calculates the ratio of errors (substitutions, insertions, and deletions) in the transcribed text to the total number of words in the reference text. A lower WER indicates better accuracy in recognizing speech

  • SI/SNR (Scale-Invariant Signal-to-Noise Ratio)

    This measures the quality of audio by comparing the clean and degraded signals. It calculates the ratio of the signal power to the noise power, adjusted to be invariant to the scale of the signals. Higher SI/SNR values indicate better audio quality
